The interface mimics the Markov-text-generator game “arXiv vs. snarXiv,” which has a random text generator that’s been trained to produce fake string theory paper titles; the game is to compare them to real paper titles from arXiv.org. This puzzle is “viXra vs sniXra,” which is a text generator—but not a random one—trained to produce fake crackpot paper titles; the “game” element is to compare them to crackpot papers from viXra.org, the “alternative archive” for amateur/outsider scientists. But as you play you’ll accumulate a list of such fake papers and their fake author lists, and even fake “sniXra” eight-digit identifiers.

If you play the game for a while you will be encouraged to look up the AIP conference proceedings identified as “4869.4736.” That’s not a real viXra identifier—the leading digits of an identifier are an upload date in YYMM format. What does it mean?

You’re meant to notice that the sniXra number is correlated with the title in some way. Maybe the easiest thing to notice is that the number of nonzero ID digits is the number of title words. You’ll also notice lots of recurring words, and that those words correlate with the ID—if the first digit is 3 then the first word is “Nuclear,” for example. If the last word is “Entropy” the last digit is 1. Indeed, each digit of the eight-bit ID corresponds to a fixed word list for that digit. If there’s a zero in the ID, no word from that place’s wordlist is present.

The authorlist may help, too. A nonzero value of the first digit will always mean Pons and Fleischmann are on the author list. A nonzero value of the 2nd digit will always mean Herndon is a coauthor.

Therefore the hinted identifier 4869.4736 would have the title “Fusion Ignition Rocket Engine With Ballistic Answer Lithium.” That happens to be almost the title of a real AIP conference proceedings:

  • FIREBALL: Fusion Ignition Rocket Engine with Ballistic Ablative Lithium Liner, AIP Conference Proceedings Volume 813(1), by Martin, Eskridge, Fimognari, and Lee

Fleischmann, Pons, and Dingle are famously connected with crackpottery. Herndon, Jovanovic, Khmelnik, Kodokula, Yie, and Beckwith are mentioned purely due to the word-content of their paper titles. The puzzle’s author has not read their papers and intends no comment. Martin, Eskridge, Fimognari, and Lee are not crackpots and I apologize for dragging them into this puzzle at all.
